Project Summary

Overview

TestTacklr is a flashcard app that provides effective study materials and various study methods to help users maximize limited study time. Users are able to create a variety of different flashcards to accomodate for all kinds of study information that do not follow the standard flashcard. There are also settings that can be customized when creating a new study set to help users stay on task and remind them of their study goals. This application also includes tools that let users review the material in different ways; not only to help drive the memorization of the material but also to keep them engaged and motivated.

Objective

With this being my first exposure to the UX world, this project introduced me to the role of being a UX designer and creating an app from scratch.

Duration

This project was self-paced, but the students were expected to complete the course within one month. I was able to complete it in two weeks.

Design Process

1. Understand

Competitor Analysis

To fully understand the scope and functionality of different flashcard applications currently available to users, I conducted a competitor analysis of Quizlet, Flash Cards, and FlashCardsGO. With this analysis, I created the spreadsheet below that consolidates information about the applications themselves, their available features, and their overall usability. From all of this information, I was able to evaluate and determine the strengths and weaknesses of each application.

Below are the features that really stood out to me from my competitor analysis. These are items that I considered incorporating in my own application.

  • A variety of ways for users to study to accommodate different learning styles
  • A community for sharing and learning
  • The ability to format cards and upload photos
  • Informative onboarding
  • An uninterrupted learning experience (minimal distractions/ads)

2. Observe

User Research

Before moving forward, I needed to know if my findings from the competitor analysis actually coincided with what my users needed. I performed additional research by interviewing college aged students on their study methods and experience with learning new vocabulary. The purpose of this was to step in the shoes of the target audience and understand their needs and goals when using technology for studying. I turned their responses into "doing," "feeling," and "thinking" statements so help summarize their thoughts and ideas.

3. Point of View

User Persona

With all of the information gathered from my user research, I created my proto-persona, Sophia. Sophia was created to be a representation of the needs, goals, behaviors and pain points of my hypothesized user.

"Sophia is a 21 year old female who is studying pre-medicine at the University of Colorado Boulder. She is a very goal driven individual who maintains a strict schedule to balance her educational life, professional life and personal life. She needs effective and efficient study methods that make the most of her study time. She wants tools that let her review the material in different ways; not only to help drive the memorization of the material but also to keep her engaged and motivated. With such a busy schedule she would benefit from something to help her stay on task and remind her of her goals.""

User Stories

To further bring Sophia to life, I created user stories to transform her basic needs into functional requests. Writing these helped me understand functions that the users will want to accomplish so I can have a better idea of how to provide a solution.

Job Stories

To take this a step further, I also created job stories. This not only helped me to understand Sophia’s needs, but also the “why” behind these needs.

Problem Statement & Hypothesis

To summarize my user research findings, user persona, user stories, and job stories, I developed a problem statement as well as a hypothesis statement. These outlined the problem I was trying to tackle along with the possible solution.

4. Ideate

Task Analysis & User Flow

Before starting my wireframes, I needed to determine how I wanted the information architecture to be structured throughout the app. Two different task analysis were mapped out to understand the step by step process required to complete a task. Since different users may take different paths to accomplish the same goal, user flows were then created to understand the various paths users may take to complete a task. I chose to outline the task flows and user flows for 1) setting the details of a flashcard set and 2) creating the flashcards themselves, as these are main functions of the app.

5. Prototype

Wireframes

With Sophia in mind and the information architecture in hand, it was time to sketch some low-fidelity wireframes. With low-fidelity wireframes I was able to focus on the foundation of the structure and navigation of my app before worrying about the visual design. I started with designing the sign in/sign up and onboarding wireframes. There were certain features that I wanted to incorporate in these wireframes, as listed below.

  • Using other accounts (i.e. Google, Facebook, Twitter) to create an account with TestTacklr. This feature can really simplify the account creation process which in turn can result in a higher number of accounts made.
  • Informative and efficient onboarding with the option to skip. In order to make sure not to lose the interest of the user, this onboarding needs to set a good foundation for the user but not be too overwhelming. Though we would like for users to go through the onboarding, they should have the option of whether or not they want to go through the onboarding process.

Now on to actually making a study set. One of the main features that Sophia needed was the ability to customize the settings of new study materials. Through our system, she can document the date of the test, set certain days to study, get reminders to study, and receive a weekly progress report. Sophia also has the option of choosing different flashcard types to accommodate for different types of materials for when she is ready to make her flashcards.

6. Test

User Testing

Now I had a prototype, but was it useful? To determine the level of usefulness, I assessed it’s level of utility and usability. Utility is characterized by the availability of features and functionality for users to complete pertinent tasks. Usability is defined by the overall user experience and their satisfaction with the application. If I achieved both of those characteristics, I created a useful app. In order to assess TestTacklr’s usefulness, I conducted usability tests with three participants over FaceTime. The test plan is further described in the table below.

Findings

The findings from user testing was analyzed with the help of Jakob Nielsen’s Error Severity Rating Scale. As I went through all of the recordings from the testing, I wrote down different errors, observations, and comments that were made by the participant. I then assigned each item a severity number ranging from zero (not a usability issue) to four (usability catastrophe). This type of characterization helped me to understand the issues that I needed to prioritize. For example, through this testing I realized I made the huge mistake of not including any back buttons. This was assigned a four because I would need to fix this before I can give it to the development team.

Final Product

Below is a list of all of the changes I made to my prototype based on the findings above. These changes helped the usability, utility, and overall usefulness of TestTacklr.

  • Having the option to “Create New Flashcards” and “Create New Test”
  • Instructions on how to create the different types of notecards
  • Information added for weekly progress report
  • Added Instagram as an upload option
  • Back buttons
  • Added more overall functionality to the prototype
